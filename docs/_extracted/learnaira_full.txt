1. INTRODUCTION  
 
Interactive learning is an educational approach that involves active 
participation and engagement from learners. It focuses on fostering a 
dynamic and collaborative environment where students can interact with 
the learning material, their peers, and their instructors. This method 
encourages hands -on experien ces, discussions, and activities that 
promote a deeper understanding of the subject matter. Interactive learning 
can take various forms, including group discussions, simulations, 
experiments, debates, and multimedia presentations, among others. 
Interactive learning has emerged as a transformative solution to 
various challenges in education.  
One prominent issue addressed by interactive learning is the prevalence 
of passive learning in traditional educational methods. Unlike conventional 
approaches that often lead to disengagement and lack of motivation 
among students, interactive learning acti vely involves students in the 
learning process. It promotes engagement, collaboration, and 
participation, encouraging students to take an active role in shaping their 
educational journey.  
Another critical problem tackled by interactive learning is the limited 
development of critical thinking skills in traditional teaching practices, 
which often prioritize rote memorization and standardized testing. 
Interactive learning environments stimulate students to analyze, evaluate, 
and apply knowledge in practical scenarios, fostering the cultivation of 
essential critical thinking and problem-solving abilities. 
Moreover, the one -size-fits-all approach in traditional classrooms, which 
fails to accommodate diverse learning styles and abilities, is effectively

1. Pros of Interactive Learning 
Interactive learning presents a myriad of benefits that contribute to a 
more dynamic and effective educational experience. One of its key 
advantages is enhanced engagement, as this approach actively involves 
students in the learning process, fostering high er levels of interest and 
participation. Consequently, students are more likely to retain knowledge 
and gain a deeper understanding of the subject matter.  
Additionally, interactive learning cultivates improved critical thinking skills

activities, providing them with the tools to analyze and evaluate information 
effectively.

instrumental in tailoring the educational experience to individual needs.

advantage, as group activities and discussions promote the development

2. Cons of Interactive Learning 
While interactive learning offers numerous advantages, it is not 
without its challenges. One notable drawback is the resource -intensive 
nature of its implementation. The integration of interactive learning often 
demands additional resources, including technology, materials, and 
trained fac ilitators. Unfortunately, these resources may not be readily 
available in all educational settings, posing a barrier to widespread 
adoption. 
Another potential drawback lies in the risk of distraction within an 
interactive learning environment. The very interactivity that promotes 
engagement can also lead to some students becoming distracted or 
disengaged, diminishing the overall effectiveness o f the learning 
experience for certain individuals. 
Unequal participation among students is another challenge associated 
with interactive learning. While some students may actively contribute to 
discussions or activities, others might be more hesitant, creating an 
imbalance in the learning process. This imbalance could hinder the overall 
development of certain students and compromise the collaborative nature 
of interactive learning. 
Technical challenges, particularly when technology is involved, represent 
another set of obstacles. Issues such as connectivity problems or software 
malfunctions can disrupt the learning process, causing frustration among 
both students and instructors. The se technical hiccups may impede the

3. PROPOSED SOLUTION  
Our proposed solution aims to create a  web-based interactive E -
Learning platform powered by artificial intelligence. This platform seeks to 
revolutionize the delivery of education, offering a comprehensive and 
dynamic environment that caters to diverse learning styles while fostering 
a safe space for collaboration, engagement, and continuous growth. 
To comprehensively address all the problems, we aim to solve and provide 
effective solutions; each issue outlined in the previous section  “Problem 
definition” will be presented with its proposed solution and a corresponding 
justification. 
Problem1: The grading of exams may not always be fair, as other factors 
can influence the outcome. 
Solution: To address this problem, our aim is to automate the evaluation 
(grading) process for students' exams. First, we will focus on multiple -
choice questions where students choose an answer from a list of provided 
options. Grading such questions is relatively straightforward, as it relies on 
an exact match between the student's answer and the reference answer. 
If the answers match precisely, full marks are awarded; otherwise, points 
are deducted. The second type of question we seek to optimize its grading 
process is the essay question. Assessing student answers for these 
questions is a challenging task due to the need to interpret the meaning of 
both the reference (correct) answer and the student's response. To 
achieve this, a deep learning model, specif ically a large -scale language 
model (LLM) like transformers, will be utilized to capture the semantic 
meaning of the answers. These meanings will be compared using a cosine 
similarity metric, analyzing embeddings generated from the model. It's

essential that the model used is trained using self -supervised techniques 
on numerous sentences to efficiently accomplish this proposed task. 
Justification: Our aim is to minimize human errors, which significantly 
impact the grading process and enhance grading accuracy. This task is 
vital for time efficiency, as it enables educators to focus more on teaching 
rather than spending extensive hours manually gradin g exams. By 
undertaking this task, we intend to eliminate favoritism and bias in grading, 
which can negatively affect this process. 
Problem2: Instructors work hard and invest a significant amount of time in 
creating exams for their students. 
Solution: Here, our aim is to assist instructors with automated question 
generation. To achieve this, we will employ a deep learning model capable 
of understanding contextual meanings and generating essay questions 
related to the information within the same context. Additionally, we will 
utilize a question -answering deep learning model to produce specific 
answers for the questions generated. These answers will serve as 
reference answers for the grading process. 
This task presents significant challenges and requires the aid of well -
trained transformers designed specifically for natural language processing. 
Moreover, a well -organized corpus must be employed and processed 
meticulously. The proposed solution will fun ction as follows: When an 
instructor needs to create an exam for a particular course, an AI -driven 
question bank will be generated using these models. The system will also 
consider chapter weight to emphasize the significance of each chapter. 
The instructor retains the flexibility to select questions from the question 
bank for the exam. Furthermore, they have the freedom to modify any 
question, its point value, and the provided answer.

Problem3: There are offensive words and hateful text in chat messages  
and reviews. 
Solution: A monitoring system will be deployed to scan all text in chat 
messages and reviews before sharing it. If any abusive text is detected, 
the content will be banned from sharing, and the sending user will be 
alerted. To enhance the efficiency of this task, an AI model will be trained 
specifically on offensive and hateful sentences to detect such content. If 
an offensive review bypasses the monitoring system, users can report it to 
the admin for appropriate action, including the deletion of any abusive text. 
Justification: This helps create a safer and more inclusive learning 
environment for students , ensuring that inappropriate or offensive 
materials are not displayed or shared within the educational community. 
Problem4: Lack of In -person Interaction between students and the  
instructors and their courses. 
Solution: We plan to employ various techniques to enhance the learning 
experience. Firstly, we will implement a hybrid question-answering system 
to address users' inquiries regarding the content. This system will leverage 
the capabilities of AI language models to provide tailored responses based 
on users' queries, thereby streamlining the process. This technique 
involves the integration of two deep learning models. The first model 
tackles the information retrieval (IR) problem, seeking the specific 
document page containing the answer. The second model utilizes the 
retrieved page (context) to accurately generate the answer using modern

generative answer extraction techniques. If the system fails to find an 
answer, it will inform the user accordingly. Otherwise, it will retrieve the

open dialogue between students and the course instructor. All messages 
will undergo filtering using AI algorithms to prevent the circulation of 
abusive content. Thirdly, a private chat option with course instructors w ill 
be made available to each student upon successful enrollment in the 
course. 
Justification: Hybrid question-answering systems offer swift and accurate 
responses to students' inquiries, aiding in their comprehension of complex 
topics more effectively. This system helps students circumvent frustration 
often experienced when sifting through extensiv e textbooks or online 
resources, streamlining the search process. Interactive question -
answering systems can significantly engage students by providing 
immediate feedback and fostering interactive learning experiences. 
Through prompt responses to queries, these systems save students time, 
enabling them to cover more topics or delve deeper into their areas of 
interest. 
Moreover, engaging in direct communication with instructors and

The incorporation of Artificial Intelligence (AI) into E -Learning

Section 1: E-Learning and Its Transformation 
1. Definition of E-Learning  
E-Learning, or Electronic Learning, utilizes digital technology, particularly 
the internet, for delivering educational content and enabling learning. It 
encompasses a broad spectrum of online educational activities, ranging 
from formal courses to self -paced learning. E -Learning is recognized for 
its transformative impact on education, offering accessibility and flexibility. 
2. Historical Context of E-Learning 
E-Learning's roots can be traced back to the early days of the internet 
when educational content was first made available online. However, it 
gained significant traction in the late 20th century with the advent of 
Learning Management Systems (LMS) and the development of online 
course platforms. E-Learning has continued to evolve over the years, with 
the integration of multimedia, interactive content, and social learning 
elements.

biases in training data, affecting learner experiences. 
Countermeasures, including algorithm audits, diverse data, and 
transparent decision -making, are crucial to reduce bias.

1.1- Overview  
Hybrid question answering system based on information retrieval 
and answer generation is an artificial intelligence, especially natural 
language processing application that combines two core functions: 
information retrieval , which involves finding and selecting relevant 
documents or passages from a vast corpus in response to a user's query, 
and answer generation , where the system extracts or generates a 
concise and contextually accurate response from the retrieved information. 
This approach integrates infor mation retrieval techniques with natural 
language generation, enhancing the system's ability to provide informative 
and contextually relevant answers to user queries, making it particularly 
useful for educational tasks.

Despite their importance and advancements, question answering tasks 
come with a variety of challenges : 
1. Ambiguity: Many questions and queries can be ambiguous, 
requiring the system to disambiguate the context and provide a 
precise answer. 
2. Lack of Context: Understanding and answering questions often 
require contextual information that may not be explicitly provided in 
the query. 
3. Handling Multi-hop Questions: Some questions necessitate 
gathering information from multiple documents or sources, which 
can be challenging for retrieval-based systems. 
4. Named Entity Recognition: Identifying and correctly 
understanding named entities in questions and documents is 
crucial for providing accurate answers. 
5. Scalability: Handling large datasets and extensive documents 
efficiently is a significant challenge for question answering systems. 
6. Real-time Interaction: For conversational or real-time question 
answering, systems need to maintain context and provide coherent 
responses. 
7. Language Variability: Variations in language, accents, or dialects 
can make it challenging to interpret and answer questions 
accurately. 
8. Lack of Training Data: Training effective question answering 
models often requires large and high-quality datasets, which may 
not always be available. 
9. Misleading Information: The presence of misleading or incorrect 
information in documents can lead to erroneous answers.

1.2 - Generative question answering  
1.2.1- Description 
 Generative question answering is an approach to question 
answering in natural language processing (NLP) where the system 
generates responses in a more open -ended, human-like manner. Unlike 
retrieval-based question answering, which selects answers from a 
predefined set of options, generative question answering systems create 
responses from scratch. These systems typically use sequence -to-
sequence models, such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs) or

contextually relevant answer in natural language. Generative question 
answering is particularly useful for answering questions that may not have 
pre-existing answers in a specific dataset an d for tasks that require more 
creative and contextual responses.  
  
 
 
 
 
 
Inputs: 
1. Question: The user's query, expressed in natural language, serves 
as the primary input. It can be a wide range of questions. 
2. Context: which provides essential background information hat help 
frame the question and provide relevant information. This context 
can be a passage of text associated with the question. 
Outputs: 
1. Answer: using advanced generative models, such as transformers 
or recurrent neural networks, the system generates an answer that 
is not limited to a predefined set of responses. Instead, it formulates 
an answer based on its understanding of the question and the 
context. This answer is a natural language response that is 
contextually relevant.

1.2.7- Modeling 
T5 is an encoder-decoder model pre-trained on a multi-task mixture 
of unsupervised and supervised tasks and for which each task is converted 
into a text-to-text format. T5 works well on a variety of tasks out-of-the-box 
by prepending a different prefix to the input corresponding to each task, 
e.g., for translation: translate English to German: …, for summarization: 
summarize: …. 
Self-supervised training uses corrupted tokens, by randomly removing 
15% of the tokens and replacing them with individual sentinel tokens (if 
several consecutive tokens are marked for removal, the whole group is 
replaced with a single sentinel token). The input of the encoder is the 
corrupted sentence, the input of the decoder is the original sentence, and 
the target is then the dropped-out tokens delimited by their sentinel tokens.

How do we train T5 model? 
T5 is an encoder-decoder model and converts all NLP problems into 
a text-to-text format. It is trained using teacher force. This means that for 
training, we always need an input sequence and a corresponding target 
sequence. The input sequence is fed to the model using  input_ids. The 
target sequence is shifted to the right, i.e., prepended by a start-sequence 
token and fed to the decoder using the  decoder_input_ids. In teacher -
forcing style, the target sequence is then appended by the EOS token and 
corresponds to the  labels. The PAD token is hereby used as the start -
sequence token. T5 can be  trained / fine-tuned both in a supervised and 
unsupervised fashion.

1.3- Information retrieval 
1.3.1- Description 
The process of information retrieval begins when a user submits a 
query into the system. These queries are formal statements of information 
needs, and in our system, we leverage them to find an answer by utilizing 
a Question Answering model. The aim is to quickly identify short text 
segments that answer the user's question within a PDF and return the page 
numbers where these answers can be found. 
Step 1: Read PDF Pages 
We start by using ‘‘pypdf[crypto]’’ to extract all pages from the PDF. 
The content of these pages is encrypted for security, and we decrypt it to 
its original form when retrieving the pages. We perform some 
preprocessing on the extracted text, such as removing newlines and extra 
whitespaces, and converting the text to lowercase. 
Step 2: Semantic Search 
Traditional search engines were lexical, matching query words 
exactly without understanding the query's meaning. Semantic Search, on 
the other hand, leverages automated natural language processing 
programs using Latent Semantic Indexing. This approach allo ws the

search engine to understand how keywords and content work together to 
convey meaning. 
We use Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA), also known as Latent 
Semantic Indexing (LSI), which is a technique in natural language 
processing that analyzes relationships between documents and the terms 
they contain. LSA identifies concepts related to the documents and terms 
by establishing associations between terms that occur in similar contexts. 
For this task, we employ the MPNet model. 
1.3.2- MPNet Model 
Pretrained language models have been a hot research topic in 
natural language processing. These models, such as BERT, are usually 
pretrained on large -scale language corpora with carefully designed 
pretraining objectives and then fine -tuned on downstream ta sks to boost 
accuracy. Among these, masked language modeling (MLM), adopted in 
BERT, and permuted language modeling (PLM), adopted in XLNet, are 
two representative pretraining objectives. However, both enjoy their own 
advantages but suffer from limitations. 
We can see that the non -predicted parts in MLM and PLM are the same, 
while the predicted parts are different. The advantage of MLM is that it can 
see the position information of the full sentence by introducing mask 
symbol [M] and its position information in the non-predicted part, while the 
advantage of PLM is that it can model the dependency among the 
predicted tokens with autoregressive generation in the predicted part. How 
can a model inherit their advantages while avoiding their limitations? Our 
work, the novel pretrained language model MPNet, uses masked and 
permuted language modeling to model the dependency among predicted 
tokens and see the position information of the full sentence.

1.4- How it’s working 
Running the QA model on multiple documents can be slow. To expedite 
the search process, you can initially employ passage ranking models to 
identify documents that may contain the answer to the question. 
Subsequently, you iterate through these documents using the QA model. 
To create a ranking model, follow these steps: 
1. Generate sentence embeddings using the MPNet Model: 
• Receive a question from the user and extract context from the 
text within a PDF, storing it as a list. 
• Add the question as the first item in the context list. 
• Apply tokenization to the context list and use the model to 
compute sentence embeddings. 
2. Calculate cosine similarity: 
• Compute the cosine similarity between the first item in the 
context list and every other item in the list. 
• Return the index with the maximum similarity, as this index likely 
contains the answer to the question. 
All system Behavior: 
• If a page contains an answer, the function returns the page number 
and the answer. 
• If a page does not contain an answer, it merges that page with the 
next one and checks again for an answer. 
• If an answer is found after merging, it returns the answer and the 
merged page number. 
• If no answer is found even after merging, it merges the page with the 
previous one (not the next) and repeats the steps to identify an 
answer or indicate no answer found.

2.1- Description 
 The detection of offensive and hate speech is an approach in natural 
language processing (NLP) that involves classifying text as offensive, 
hateful, or normal. In this process, the system determines whether the text 
is offensive or not. These systems typi cally employ algorithms such as 
Support Vector Machines (SVM), Naive Bayes, or deep learning 
architectures like Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and Transformers. 
The model learns patterns and relationships between the extracted 
features and the offensive or non-offensive labels. Challenges may arise 
when detecting nuanced or context -dependent offensive language, and 
the accuracy of such systems can vary across different languages, 
domains, or cultural contexts. To ensure the appropriate handling of 
flagged content and to address potential false positives or false negatives 
generated by the system, human review and intervention remain crucial. 
Despite their importance and advancements, Detection of offensive and 
hate speech tasks come with a variety of challenges: 
1- Contextual Understanding: Offensive language and hate speech 
can be highly context -dependent, relying on cultural references, 
sarcasm, irony, or subtle cues. Interpreting text accurately requires 
understanding the contextual nuances, which can be challenging for 
an automated system. 
 
2- Evolving Language and New Terminologies: Offensive language 
and hate speech can evolve rapidly, with new terms, slurs, or coded 
language constantly emerging. Keeping up with these changes and 
updating the system's vocabulary and knowledge base presents a 
challenge.

2.4- Text cleaning 
 Text data often contains various forms of noise, such as special 
characters, punctuation, extra whitespace, or irrelevant symbols. Cleaning 
helps remove these unwanted elements, making the text more suitable for 
analysis. Text cleaning helps to standardize the format of text data. Text 
data can carry biases or stereotypes. Cleaning can involve the removal or 
replacement of such content, ensuring that the NLP model doesn't 
perpetuate harmful biases. Cleaned text can lead to better model 
performance. NLP models often work more effectively when they are 
trained on clean, standardized data. So, the follow ing steps was adopted 
to clean text without changing its meaning: 
1- text = re.sub("(@[A -Za-z0-9]+)", " ",text) Removing Handles 
(@[A-Za-z0-9]+): handles are replaced with a space. This is 
important when working with social media text data where mentions 
might not be relevant for text classification tasks. 
2- Removing URLs: URLs, typically of the form https://example.com,  
are replaced with a space. This helps eliminate web links that may 
not provide meaningful information for classification.  
3- Removing Hashtags (#\w)**: Hashtags, such as "#example," are 
removed to avoid the classification model considering them as 
distinct tokens. Often, the content of hashtags is integrated into the 
text and is more informative when treated as regular words. 
4- Removing Special Characters:  All special characters (except 
digits, letters, spaces, and tabs) are replaced with a space. This 
helps ensure that the text consists of meaningful words and 
punctuation.

5- Removing Extra White Spaces: Multiple consecutive white spaces 
are replaced with a single space. This step ensures that the text is 
consistent and free from excessive spaces. 
6- Lowercasing: The text is converted to lowercase to ensure case 
insensitivity when performing text classification. This avoids treating 
"word" and "Word" as distinct words.

2.7-Modeling 
❖ Design 
BERT is an "encoder -only" transformer architecture. 
On a high level, BERT consists of three modules: 
• Embedding: This module converts an array of one -hot encoded 
tokens into an array of vectors representing the tokens. 
• a stack of encoders:  These encoders are the Transformer 
encoders. They perform transformations over the array of 
representation vectors. 
• un-embedding: This module converts the final representation 
vectors into one-hot encoded tokens again. 
The un -embedding module is necessary for pretraining, but it is often 
unnecessary for downstream tasks. Instead, one would take the 
representation vectors output at the end of the stack of encoders and use 
those as a vector representation of the text input and train a smaller model 
on top of that.

BERT uses WordPiece to convert each English word into an integer code. 
Its vocabulary has size 30,000. Any token not appearing in its vocabulary 
is replaced by [UNK] for "unknown". 
❖ Pretraining 
BERT was pre-trained simultaneously on two tasks:  
1- language modeling: 15% of tokens were selected for prediction, and 
the training objective was to predict the selected token given its context. 
The selected token is:  
• replaced with a [MASK] token with probability 80%, 
• replaced with a random word token with probability 10%, 
• not replaced with probability 10%. 
For example, the sentence "my dog is cute" may have the 4 -th token 
selected for prediction. The model would have input text:  
• "My dog is [MASK]" with probability 80%, 
• "My dog is happy" with probability 10%, 
• "My dog is cute" with probability 10%. 
 
After processing the input text, the model's 4-th output vector is passed to 
a separate neural network, which outputs a probability distribution over its 
30,000-large vocabulary.

3- AUTOMATIC SPEECH RECOGNITION  
3.1- Description 
  
 Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) is a transformative technology 
designed to convert spoken language into accurate written text. In the 
evolution of ASR systems, a historical reliance on models such as Hidden 
Markov Models (HMMs) and Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) prevailed. 
However, in recent years, a noteworthy paradigm shift has occurred within 
the field, steering away from traditional methodologies and gravitating 
towards the adoption of advanced deep learning techniques. Notably, 
transformer-based mod els, originally introduced for natural language 
processing tasks, have emerged as a focal point in the modernization of 
ASR. These transformers, with their distinctive self-attention mechanisms, 
prove to be highly effective in capturing intricate patterns and long-range 
dependencies within the audio signal. This transition towards deep 
learning signifies a departure from rule-based systems to data-driven, end-
to-end learning approaches, contributing significantly to the enhanced 



===== PAGE 250 =====

performance and adaptability of ASR systems across diverse linguistic 
contexts and real-world applications.  
 ASR offers several benefits for users dealing with online platforms, 
especially those designed for interactive learning. This technology tends 
to highly engage students in the learning process:  
• Enhanced Accessibility:  ASR facilitates inclusive education by 
providing a voice interface, allowing students with diverse learning 
needs, including those with disabilities, to interact with the learning 
platform more effectively. 
• Engagement and Motivation : The interactive nature of ASR in 
learning platforms captivates students' attention and fosters 
engagement. Gamification elements, interactive dialogues, and real-
world language applications contribute to a more motivating learning 
environment. 
 The Transformer model comprises a single large block, which, in 
turn, consists of blocks of encoders and decoders. In this context, the 
encoder takes the feature vectors from the audio signal X = (x1, ..., xT) as 
input and produces a sequence of intermedia te representations. 
Subsequently, based on these received representations, the decoder 
generates the output sequence W = wm = (w1, ..., wM). Each stage of the 
model utilizes preceding symbols to generate the next one, as it is 
autoregressive. The Transformer architecture incorporates multiple layers 
of self -attention in both the encoder and decoder blocks, establishing 
interconnectedness. 
An encoder block consists of sets of encoders which are located one 
above the other. All encoders have the same structure but different 
weights. The input of the encoder receives extracted feature vectors from 
the audio signal, obtained using Mel -frequency cepstral coefficients or 


===== PAGE 251 =====

251 
convolutional neural networks. Then the first encoder transforms these 
data using self-attention into a set of vectors, and through the feed forward 
ANN transmits the received outputs to the next encoder. The last encoder 
processes the vectors and transfer s the data of the encoded functions to 
the decoder block. 
A decoder block is a set of decoders, and their number is usually 
identical to the number of encoders. Each part of the encoder can be 
divided into two sublayers: the input data entering the encoder first 
passes through the multi-head attention layer, which helps the encoder 
look at other words in the incoming sentence during encoding of a 
particular word. The output of the inner multi-head attention layer is sent 
to the feed-forward neural network. The exact same network is 
independently applied to each word in the sentence. 
The decoder also contains two of these layers, but there is an attention 
layer between them that helps the decoder focus on significant parts of the 
incoming sentence, as is like the usual attention mechanism in seq2seq 
models. This component will consider  previous characters/words and 
based on these data, outputs the posterior probabilities of the subsequent 
character/words. 
The Transformer model includes Scaled Dot-Product Attention. The 
advantage of self -attention is fast calculation and shortening of the path 
between words, as well as potential interpretability. 
In the Transformer architecture, you can see the Normalize element, which 
is necessary to normalize feature values, since after using the attention 
mechanism, these values can have different values.

3.4- Evaluation 
 For this type of model which generates a sequence of words, the 
used metric must differ from traditional classification/regression models, so 
we used “BLEU” score here for evaluation process. 
  The BLEU (Bilingual Evaluation Understudy) score is a numerical 
measure that quantifies the similarity between an automated system's 
output and one or more reference translations. It operates by comparing 
n-grams (contiguous sequences of n items, usually words) in the 
generated text with those in the reference texts. The score ranges from 0 
to 1, with higher scores indicating greater similarity and better 
performance. BLEU is widely used for its simplicity and effectiveness in 
providing a quick evaluation of the quality of machine-generated text.

 In pursuit of achieving this task, a systematic series of steps was 
implemented to successfully extract written text from the sound wave 
acquired from the user. It is crucial to highlight that the core of these

in Python, a specialized module for efficient audio preprocessing.

1. Load audio and get sample rate. 
2. Normalize the audio volume to the scale of the used model. 
3. Resample the audio to a common frequency (16000 Hz) which used 
during model training. 
4. Flatten the audio tensor and ensure it's a 1D tensor. 
5. Tokenize and prepare the input for the ASR model.
